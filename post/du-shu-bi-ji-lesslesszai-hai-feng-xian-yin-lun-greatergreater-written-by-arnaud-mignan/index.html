Chapter1. OVERVIEW OF CATASTROPHE RISKS

Concept of catastrophe

sudden natural or man-made events of grave consequence
We use the term peril to label those dierent types of events

Even though there is a tendency towards a catastrophe being of wider impact than a disaster, disaster is the same like catastrophe.

Concept of risk

"Risk may be defined as a compound measure of the probability and magnitude of adverse effect" (Lowrance, 1980, p. 5).

Risk assessment: This is the process of identifying and evaluating risks.

Risk management: This consists of the numerous ways of dealing with risk, such as reducing it or transferring it (e.g. land use planning, building retrofitting, insurance), as well as with post-catastrophe damage reparation (Lowrance, 1980, p. 12).

Risk governance: This encompasses all of the above, as well as the many interactions between different stakeholders (governmental, corporate and individual decision-making).

Hazard module: Characterises the source of events of different magnitudes expected in a specified spatiotemporal window for a given peril, as well as the frequency and intensity of those events.
For earthquakes, the source is a fault segment and the intensity the level of ground shaking; for storms, the source is the projected path and the intensity the wind speed.

Exposure module: Characterises the inventory of buildings and other infrastructures (or portfolio of properties and other assets in insurance terminology) at risk. The main parameters are the location and structure characteristics, such as construction material, number of stories, age, etc..

Vulnerability module: Computes the vulnerability or susceptibility to damage of the structures at risk, by combining the outputs of the hazard and exposure modules. This requires a database of vulnerability functions and/or fragility curves relating damage level to hazard intensity and the structures' characteristics.

Loss module: Evaluates the losses from the measures of vulnerability. This module outputs the main results of the model, under the form of different risk metrics. Losses are often defined in economic terms (monetary value) or human terms (fatalities, sometimes also injuries). Such units of loss make it possible to compare risks on a uniform basis. it is also relatively well quantified for past disasters for model calibration and validation. Another loss quantity which may be considered is shortening of lifespan (Schwing, 1980:134).
